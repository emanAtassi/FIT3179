Electricity and Clean Energy Across the World

In an era defined by progress and innovation, the global demand for electricity has surged to unprecedented heights. Electricity, often referred to as the lifeblood of modern civilization, powers our homes, industries, and economies, enabling the conveniences and advancements we often take for granted. However, this remarkable energy source comes with a significant responsibilityâ€”its production and consumption can impact our planet's health and sustainability. This is where the concept of clean energy enters the stage.

Clean energy, a term synonymous with environmental stewardship, represents a pivotal shift in the way we generate and harness electricity. It champions sustainability, striving to reduce our reliance on fossil fuels and mitigate the harmful emissions they produce. Clean energy sources encompass a wide array of technologies, from wind and solar power to hydropower, geothermal energy, and beyond. These sources not only reduce our carbon footprint but also offer the promise of a greener, more sustainable future.

The journey towards clean energy is multifaceted, involving international cooperation, policy changes, technological advancements, and public awareness. It transcends borders, as countries worldwide seek to balance their growing energy needs with the imperative to protect our environment. From initiatives to improve access to electricity in underserved regions to ambitious renewable energy goals, the global community is forging a path towards a more sustainable and electrified world.

In the pages that follow, it explores a series of visualizations that illuminate the diverse facets of electricity and clean energy on a global scale. These visual insights offer a deeper understanding of the global use of electricity consumption and sources, and compares the progress of countries' progress, or lack thereof, of leading a sustainable world.

The data used in this visualisation ranges from 2000 to 2020, or 2019 for some charts. Please view the page in full view to get the best display!

Access to Electricity Across the World (%):

The chart below is a choropleth map, which displays the % of individuals that have access to electricity per country. The slider below allows for filtering the values by year. Move the slider through to see the progress of countries' accessibility to electricity! The map scale allows for zooming into the map to get a more detailed look.

The colour scale used denotes the % of access to electricity, where the darker the colour, the higher the %. Note that some countries are in grey due to missing data.

As we can see from the map, most countries, if not all, have experienced a positive improvement from 2000 in terms of citizens having access to electricity. Especially Afghanistan for instance; it improved drastically from 2% to 97%! These changes indicate electricity has become profoundly more accessible in the past two decades, and invites hope that it will only become more widely accessible in the future.

Financial Aid ($US) to Developing Counrties vs Access to Clean Fuels (%):

This is a proportional symbol map, which displays the financial aid received by developing countries (in $US) vs their access to clean fuels as a %. Clean fuels refers to fuel that is safe for consumption, such as for cooking. The size of the circles denotes the average amount of financial aid received in that particular year per 10,000 persons. The colours denote the % of clean fuels accessed by citizens of a particular country.

Likewise to the above graph, there is a year slider that filters data by the chosen year, as well as a map scale that allows for zooming into the map. Zoom in to get a closer and clearer look at the data!

Overall, it can be said from the graph that the bigger the circles, the darker the colour, which indicates receiving financial aid may assist in having more access to clean fuels for use. However, there are many countries whre despite receiving a substantial amount of aid, they still lack in access to clean fuels. This indicates that, in fact, the correlation between financial aid and accessing clean fuels is not very strong.

Despite receiving financial aid for clean energy initiatives, many countries in regions like Africa face persistent challenges in achieving widespread access to clean fuels. Factors such as inadequate infrastructure, economic constraints, political instability, and a lack of technical expertise can impede progress. Additionally, cultural preferences, energy subsidies, and global energy dynamics play a role.

Anuual GDP Per Capita vs Renewable Energy Consumption:




This is a bubble chart that displays GDP Per Capita against renewable electricity generation per person, which describes the installed renewable energy capacity per person. Each circle represents a country, and the size of the circles denotes the population of said country. Moreover, the colour scheme denotes the continent to which the country belongs.

There is a year slider that allows for filtering the data by a specific year. There is also a checkbox if you want to review the entire data for all years. Lastly, you can select to filter the data by a specific continent.

We can see that there a positive correlation between the GDP and Renwable Energy, which highlights that the higher-income countries, the more renewable energy being installed is, whereas for lower-income countries, they may still be reliant on cheaper, yet less sustainable energy sources such as fossil fuels. See the below chart to more detailed information regarding this.

Generation of Electricity by Sources Over the Years:




This is a normalised stacked bar chart, which displays and compares the use of energy sources of fossil fuels, nuclear and renewables for each year from 2000 to 2020 as portions of a whole. You can select a specific country to view its use of electricity for each year.

Overall, we can see that fossil fuels, despite their detrimental harm on the environment, are the dominant source of electricity due to their historical infrastructure, reliability, high energy density, and cost-effectiveness in many regions. Additionally, energy security and political influence play significant roles in their continued use.

If you filter through the countries, we can see that Asian, African and American countries suffer the most from lack of clean energy like nuclear and renewables. On the other hand, European countries such as Sweden and Switzerland dominate in their use of nuclear and renewable electricity.

The variation in energy source usage among countries stems from a blend of historical, geographic, economic, and policy factors. For instance, early investments in clean energy, geographical access to resources, economic development levels, government policies, and cultural attitudes all play a role. Some countries have abundant renewable resources and prioritize them, while others rely on fossil fuels due to economic constraints, energy security concerns, or historical infrastructure. Global commitments and environmental awareness are also pushing many nations toward cleaner energy sources, reflecting a complex interplay of influences.

Countries that have reduced their CO2 Emissions from 2000 to 2019 (By continent):

The below dot charts display the countries that belong to each continent (i.e. in each chart), where the circles represent the country, and the colour represents whether a country has reduced its CO2 emissions from 2000 to 2019. Colour blue indicates that it reduced emissions, while the colour grey indicates the opposite.

As expected from the above chart, European countries perform signficantly better in comparison with all the continents, whilst all other continents' countries still have progress to make on reducing emissions and embracing better sustainable practices.

The Top 5 vs The Rest:



This is a stacked area chart which compares the CO2 Emissions produced by the Top 10 Pollutors (countries) vs the Rest of the World. You can use the year slider to filter the years, and get closer year intervals for more detail.

Countries like China, United Status and India have dominated in their carbon emissions for years now, as can be shown in the years displayed on the chart. These top countries, in comparison to hundreds of other, produce over 3 times as much as the rest of the world! and their emissions are increasing by the year.

Potential reasons for their notably higher carbon emissions compared to other countries are due to their large populations, rapid industrialization, diverse economies, historical reliance on fossil fuels, and ongoing transition to cleaner energy sources. These factors, along with high energy consumption driven by industries and urbanization, contribute to their significant carbon footprints on the global scale.
